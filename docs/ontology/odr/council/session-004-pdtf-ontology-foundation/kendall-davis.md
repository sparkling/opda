# Kendall + Davis — Enterprise-pair position on S004

## Pair summary (≤100 words)

The Foundation as drafted reads as a faithful synthesis of S001's rulings: hash namespace, layer-segregated naming, three-graph separation, generator-first, glossary-anchored sourcing. We endorse the substance and split on emphasis. Kendall (FIBO Modelling Team Process) anchors the depth on Q3 (build-step graph composition) and Q4 (precedence: W3C > glossary > schema text, conflict logged in module Change Log). Davis (BBC `/programmes/`, data.gov.uk linked-data cookbook) anchors Q5 (build-and-commit generator output, the BBC pattern) and Q7 (namespace string + calendar/semantic versioning split). Joint stance: ratify with strong defaults on the WG-owned strings.

## Per-question positions

### Q1 — Hash vs slash

**Kendall:** FIBO's published practice splits exactly along the size axis. The Foundation/AnnotationVocabulary modules — `fibo-fnd-utl-av/AnnotationVocabulary.rdf` and its siblings — are hash-fragmented because every term they declare belongs together in one whole-document fetch (FIBO OMG Specification 2024-Q4, §AnnotationVocabulary module manifest). The larger production modules (Securities, Derivatives) move to slash with content negotiation because the per-term documentation budget justifies the operational cost. OPDA's TBox at Foundation scope is the textbook FIBO `fnd-utl-av` case: small whole-document fetch, single canonical hash namespace, no per-term content negotiation infrastructure to maintain. Hash is correct.

The operational consequence worth pinning: with hash URIs the OPDA web team's commitment is to serve *one* document at `opda.uk/ns/` (Turtle, RDF/XML, or with content negotiation between them) — not to serve a separate document per term. That maps to a single static-file serve, which the OPDA Astro/Cloudflare Pages site already supports trivially. Slash would require per-term routing and content negotiation infrastructure OPDA has not yet built and does not need to build for a TBox of this size.

**Davis:** Concur, and stronger. BBC `/programmes/` used slash *because* it served tens of millions of individual programme resources, each with its own representation, content negotiation, redirect lineage, and persistent cool-URI commitment (BBC Programmes Ontology v1.1, `bbc.co.uk/ontologies/po/`; "BBC Programmes Ontology" Raimond, Smethurst, McParland 2009). That was an ABox decision driven by scale, not a TBox decision driven by elegance. The OPDA TBox at this stage has dozens to a few hundred terms — orders of magnitude under the BBC `/programmes/` threshold. Hash satisfies the linked-data principles (Berners-Lee 2006, principles 1–3) for a whole-document TBox of this size without the operational cost slash imposes.

Note for the WG: the BBC ontology itself (`bbc.co.uk/ontologies/po/`) used hash for its TBox — `bbc.co.uk/ontologies/po/Programme`, `bbc.co.uk/ontologies/po/Brand`, etc. with `#` fragmentation — even while the *instance* URIs at `/programmes/{pid}` used slash. That split is exactly the design pattern OPDA should adopt: hash for the TBox; slash for the (eventual) ABox at instance scale. The ODR draft already implicitly licenses this by declaring an instance-URI *pattern* (`…/property/uprn/{n}`) without minting instances this round (Rule 5).

### Q2 — Layer-segregated naming

**Kendall:** FIBO uses *module-prefix* layer separation rather than naming-convention-within-a-flat-namespace — `fibo-fnd-rel-rel:` for Foundations/Relations, `fibo-be-le-lei:` for Business Entities/Legal Entities/LEI, and so on. The Kind/Role distinction at FIBO is encoded by *which module a term lives in* (Foundations vs Business Entities vs Securities), not by a CamelCase convention on a flat prefix. OPDA's draft choice — flat `opda:` namespace with naming convention — is the analogue *for a small ontology that lacks FIBO's institutional module-management discipline*. Hendler's S001 framing — that the discipline is enforced via review rather than via URL structure — is the load-bearing point. Naming convention works *if* the review gate is real; without it, the layer separation degrades to wishful thinking. We endorse the draft with that caveat noted.

The Kind/Role naming convention worth specifying explicitly: Kinds carry their identity-criterion name in singular CamelCase (`opda:Property`, `opda:Person`, `opda:RegisteredTitle`); Roles carry their participant-relationship name in singular CamelCase with a relator suffix only when needed for disambiguation (`opda:Seller`, `opda:Buyer`, `opda:Proprietor` — singular; never `opda:SellerRole` because the role-vs-Kind distinction is supposed to be carried by the linguistic form, not by a suffix). FIBO does the same: `fibo-be-le-lp:Person` (Kind) vs `fibo-be-le-lp:Proprietor` (Role) — no `*Role` suffix; the linguistic form does the work. The naming convention is reviewable by anyone reading the prefix; the enforcement is by ODR review.

**Davis:** Flat namespace with naming convention worked at BBC and data.gov.uk for 15 years (`bbc.co.uk/ontologies/po/` declared `Programme`, `Brand`, `Series`, `Episode` flat, with the type hierarchy encoded by `rdfs:subClassOf`, not by URL path). The data.gov.uk linked-data cookbook §3 made the same call explicit: deeply-nested module URL hierarchies constrain editorial reorganisation and impose a renaming cost every time scope shifts (the BBC Music ontology, originally separate, was eventually re-rooted under `/ontologies/mo/` after a year of edits — the renames were painful). For OPDA's first release at TBox scale, flat-and-flexible beats hierarchical-and-rigid. The enforcement is by review, as Hendler said.

One BBC operational note: the `po:` prefix encoded layer membership by *adjacency* in the documentation (the BBC published a single page per ontology with all terms listed in layer order), not by URL. Readers learned the Kind/Role distinction from the documentation; the URLs were stable identifiers. OPDA can do the same: publish a single ontology page at `opda.uk/ns/` listing terms grouped by layer (Kinds first, Roles second, Relators third, etc.), and let the documentation carry the layer signal that the URLs do not.

### Q3 — Three-graph separation (KENDALL DEPTH)

**Kendall:** FIBO's Production reasoning profile is the canonical worked example. The Production profile keeps the class graph (OWL EL or OWL 2 RL, depending on the module) separate from the validation graph (SHACL shapes for downstream tooling) and from the advisory annotation graph (rendering hints, deprecation markers, mapping notes). The class graph is reasoned over; the shapes graph is closed-world-validated against instances; the annotation graph is presentational. Each lives in its own artefact, imported separately (or referenced without import — see below) by the consuming tool. This is FIBO Modelling Team Process §3.4 (graph separation) and is enforced at FIBO's editorial review gate — reviewers reject pull requests that introduce `owl:imports` from the shapes graph to the class graph.

Why three graphs rather than two? The advisory annotation graph is the load-bearing separation FIBO learned the hard way. Early FIBO releases mingled rendering hints (`fibo-fnd-utl-av:abbreviation`, `fibo-fnd-utl-av:explanatoryNote`) with constraint shapes in the same file; downstream consumers reasoning over the class graph occasionally surfaced presentational annotations as if they were ontological commitments. The fix was to exile presentational content to a third graph keyed to the same class IRIs but kept out of the reasoning pass. OPDA's S001 Q5 resolution — `opda:aiHint` exiled to a separate annotation graph keyed to shape IRIs — is the same pattern, arrived at by the same route (Cagle vs Knublauch/Gandon). The Foundation ODR codifies it as the discipline; ODR-0010 will operationalise the keying convention.

Composition discipline: at *build* time, the class graph and the shapes graph are concatenated into a single RDF dataset that the SHACL processor reads. `sh:targetClass` references the class IRI; the SHACL processor resolves the reference by reading the class graph from the same dataset. This is a *build-step graph-union* — operationally trivial (concatenation; no semantic transformation), philosophically sharp (the class graph remains imports-free of the shapes graph; the shapes graph remains imports-free of the class graph; both happen to be present in the same dataset for the SHACL run).

Worked example to make the composition concrete. OPDA Foundation ships:

- `foundation.ttl` — the OWL/RDFS class graph: `opda:Property a owl:Class ; rdfs:label "Property" ; dct:source <…glossary#Property>`.
- `foundation-shapes.ttl` — the SHACL shapes graph: `opda:PropertyShape a sh:NodeShape ; sh:targetClass opda:Property ; sh:property [ sh:path opda:uprn ; sh:datatype xsd:string ]`.
- `foundation-annotations.ttl` — the advisory graph: `opda:Property opda:aiHint "Refer by UPRN where present" .`

The validator's input is `foundation.ttl ∪ foundation-shapes.ttl` (set union via concatenation). The shapes graph contains no `owl:imports`; it relies on the SHACL processor to read both graphs from the dataset. The advisory graph is loaded only by consumers that explicitly want rendering hints — never by the validator, never by the reasoner.

For *query* time the discipline is the same: the consumer loads both graphs into one SPARQL endpoint and queries across them. The class graph holds the open-world TBox; the shapes graph holds the closed-world constraints; the query mixes them with the SPARQL engine acting as the integration layer. `owl:imports` from shapes to classes is forbidden — this is exactly what ODR-0002's new `### Reference-not-import` subsection codifies. The FIBO discipline (`fibo-fnd-utl-av` references `dct:` without `owl:imports`) is the precedent. Importing creates an entailment-chain dependency the shapes graph does not need; reference-only preserves the separation.

There is a third reason the separation matters: SHACL processors and OWL reasoners have *different* model theories. An OWL reasoner reading a shapes graph that asserts `sh:minCount 1` may try to entail something about the class (it cannot, but the processing cost is non-zero). A SHACL processor reading a class graph that asserts `owl:cardinality 1` may try to validate it as a shape (it cannot, but the error noise is real). Keeping the graphs separate keeps the model theories separate — the OWL reasoner reads only the class graph; the SHACL processor reads class + shapes; neither has to filter the other's vocabulary.

**Davis:** Concur with build-step composition. At BBC and data.gov.uk we used the same pattern under different names — the build pipeline produced multiple TTL artefacts (the ontology, the rendering metadata, the dataset record) and the consuming application read whichever subset it needed. Runtime `owl:imports` added operational fragility we never tolerated: a broken import URL took down validation. Reference-not-import is the operationally robust pattern.

One operational note from data.gov.uk: when we did mix graphs in the early days (2010-era catalogue records that included both DCAT and `vocab:` annotations in the same file), the maintainability cost was higher than the storage savings. Splitting into separate artefacts and using the build pipeline to compose was a one-week investment that paid back within months. OPDA should make the split from day one, not retrofit it after the first overlay profile ships.

### Q4 — Term-sourcing precedence (KENDALL DEPTH)

**Kendall:** FIBO Modelling Team Process Chapter 4 (Term Provenance) names the precedence rule almost verbatim: external W3C/OMG/ISO specification > business glossary (the EDM Council Glossary in FIBO's case) > schema-leaf annotation. The rationale: a term whose semantics are governed by an external standard MUST defer to that standard's definition; restating the W3C definition in an OPDA file invites drift. Where the glossary refines the definition (adding domain-specific scope), the refinement is recorded as a `rdfs:comment` extension, not a replacement of the canonical definition. Where the schema-leaf annotation conflicts with the glossary, the glossary wins and the schema annotation is logged as a *conflict record* in the relevant module ODR's Change Log.

The mechanical encoding: every minted term carries `dct:source` pointing to the *most authoritative* source per the precedence — i.e. if the term is governed by a W3C spec, `dct:source` points at the spec URI (the canonical fragment, e.g. `https://www.w3.org/TR/vc-data-model-2.0/#dfn-verifiable-credential`); if governed by the glossary, at the glossary row's IRI (`source/00-deliverables/semantic-models/business-glossary.ttl#TrustFramework`); if neither, at the canonical schema-leaf path. Where the glossary row diverges from the W3C spec, the glossary row carries a `rdfs:seeAlso` to the W3C source and a Change Log note in the relevant module ODR explaining the divergence. This is FIBO's `fibo-fnd-utl-av:adaptedFrom` / `fibo-fnd-utl-av:explanatoryNote` pattern applied to OPDA's term economy.

Worked example to make the precedence concrete. Take `opda:Issuer` (a participant role in the verifiedClaims layer). The W3C VC Data Model 2.0 §3.4 defines `Issuer` (`https://www.w3.org/TR/vc-data-model-2.0/#dfn-issuer`); the OPDA business glossary defines `Issuer` for the trust-framework context (which may overlap or extend); the PDTF schema annotation defines `issuer` in the verifiedClaims envelope (which is a faithful echo of the W3C term). Precedence: the W3C spec wins. `opda:Issuer` carries `dct:source <https://www.w3.org/TR/vc-data-model-2.0/#dfn-issuer>` and `rdfs:label "Issuer"` with `skos:definition` *quoting* the W3C spec rather than restating. If the glossary adds OPDA-specific scope ("an Issuer in the OPDA trust framework is a regulated participant per the AML scheme"), that scope appears as a `rdfs:comment` extension with a Change Log entry explaining the refinement. The W3C definition remains canonical; the glossary annotation is additive.

Counter-case: take `opda:TrustFramework` (an OPDA-specific concept with no W3C governance). The OPDA business glossary defines it (per the glossary's 54-term trust-framework portion); the PDTF schema does not. Precedence: the glossary wins. `opda:TrustFramework` carries `dct:source <…business-glossary.ttl#TrustFramework>` and the glossary row IS the canonical definition. No W3C-spec annotation; no Change Log entry (no divergence to record). This is the "domain-only" case that data.gov.uk's cookbook also addresses.

Edge case worth pinning: take `opda:LEI` (a Legal Entity Identifier). The ISO 17442 standard defines LEI as a 20-character alphanumeric code with check digits and a registration authority hierarchy. Precedence: ISO 17442 wins over the glossary, which wins over schema-leaf annotation. `opda:LEI` carries `dct:source <ISO 17442>` (or the GLEIF technical reference if ISO standard URI is paywalled), and its datatype constraints reflect the ISO 17442 format. This is how FIBO handles `fibo-fnd-le-lei:LegalEntityIdentifier` (FIBO LE module §LEI); OPDA inherits the pattern.

The conflict-logging discipline is load-bearing because it forces conflicts into the open. Silent divergence between glossary and W3C spec is the failure mode FIBO has spent twelve years guarding against — and it remains the most common pull-request-rejection reason at the FIBO MTP. ODR-0004's rule 7 already names the precedence; the amendment we'd add is: *conflicts are recorded as Change Log rows in the relevant module ODR, with the divergence explained and the precedence applied*. This applies downstream by ODR-0008, ODR-0011, ODR-0013 as already noted.

**Davis:** Concur. The data.gov.uk linked-data cookbook §2 (vocabulary selection) named the same precedence in different language: "for terms governed by an external standard, use the standard's URI as canonical; for terms in your own domain, mint locally with `dct:source` to the most authoritative internal definition." At data.gov.uk the precedence was: W3C/ISO > ONS / departmental glossary > schema-leaf comment. Same rule, different domain. Glossary-driven labels with schema-leaf fallback is the right discipline for OPDA because the trust framework's ubiquitous-language authority IS the business glossary (per ODR-0001 §Project Weighting and the adoption record). The schema-leaf annotation is documentation accident; the glossary is intent.

One data.gov.uk learning worth noting for OPDA's WG: when departmental teams disagreed with the canonical W3C/ISO definition (e.g. ONS wanted a UK-specific narrowing of an ISO 3166 country code definition), the resolution was *always* to keep the canonical definition canonical and record the UK refinement as a glossary extension — never to redefine the term. The reason: every consumer pulling RDF from data.gov.uk eventually had to reconcile with consumers pulling from other linked-data sources, and the only way to keep that reconciliation tractable was to anchor on the canonical definitions. OPDA will face the same pressure (especially when integrating with HMLR, ONS, or future EU regulators); building the precedence discipline in now prevents the retrofit cost later.

### Q5 — Generator-first policy (DAVIS DEPTH)

**Davis:** The BBC `/programmes/` precedent is exactly this pattern. The `/programmes/` RDF was *generated from a TV listings database* (the corporate "Programme Information & Programme Support" — PIPS — system), not hand-authored. The build pipeline ran nightly: read PIPS, produce RDF per programme/series/brand/episode, publish to `bbc.co.uk/programmes/{pid}.rdf`. The generator was the source of truth; the RDF was the build artefact; the *configuration* of the generator (which database fields became which RDF predicates) was the human-curated part, checked into version control. Documented in: Raimond, Smethurst, McParland 2009, "BBC Programmes Ontology"; subsequent presentations at SemTechBiz 2010 by Smethurst describing the nightly-build pipeline.

For OPDA: the same pattern. The data dictionary (`data-dictionary-canonical.json`, 1,557 leaves, 935 annotated) is the source of truth for the mechanical half. The generator is the transformer — it reads the data dictionary and produces the `DatatypeProperty` Turtle with `xsd:` ranges, `rdfs:domain` from the enclosing object, `rdfs:comment` from the annotation, `dct:source` to the canonical leaf path. The TTL output is the build artefact. Run the generator in CI on data-dictionary changes; commit the output to the same repo for review; the diff is the review surface. This is the workflow that worked at BBC and data.gov.uk; it is the workflow that survives over a multi-year programme without the generator becoming undocumented tribal knowledge.

Three operational notes worth pinning explicitly:

1. **Output checked into version control.** The generated TTL is committed to the repo, not regenerated on every fetch. This makes diffs reviewable, lets git history be the audit trail, and prevents reproducibility regressions when the generator changes. BBC `/programmes/` followed this discipline; data.gov.uk did the same for its DCAT records.
2. **Generator is human-edited; output is not.** The generator's configuration (the mapping rules) is the locus of human deliberation. The output Turtle is read-only — any divergence between generator output and committed output fails CI. This is what makes the generator-first policy auditable.
3. **Generator handles the mechanical; humans handle the ambiguous.** Allemang's S001 Q1 framing is right: the generator owns the 935 annotated leaves that are mechanical (named slot → `DatatypeProperty` with `xsd:` range and `rdfs:comment` from the annotation); humans own the aggregate boundaries, the cross-overlay synonymies, the `oneOf`-as-subclass-vs-state calls. The handoff is by ODR — the generator's output is what subsequent module ODRs read and refine; module ODRs MAY override specific generator outputs with hand-authored content where the genuinely ambiguous calls demand it.

For OPDA's CI: the generator should run on PRs that touch the data dictionary OR the generator's configuration. The diff between regenerated output and committed output is the review surface. If they match, the PR passes; if they diverge, the PR shows the new committed TTL and reviewers see exactly what changed. This is the BBC pattern adapted for git — at BBC the nightly build wrote to a separate publishing repo; for OPDA, the simpler approach is to commit to the same repo with CI enforcing match.

Worth noting: the BBC nightly-build pipeline had failure modes the OPDA generator should design against. The two most common: (a) the PIPS schema changed in a way that broke the generator's assumptions, producing malformed RDF; the fix was schema-version pinning and a smoke test that ran the generator against a frozen sample dataset before each release. (b) The generator's output drifted over time as the BBC ontologists hand-edited the generated RDF for "just this one case"; the fix was banning hand-edits to output and treating any divergence as a generator-config update. OPDA should adopt both disciplines from day one — schema-version pinning between data-dictionary and generator-config, and a no-hand-edits-to-output rule enforced by CI.

**Kendall:** FIBO does not follow generator-first — FIBO's authoring discipline is *ontology-IDE-driven* (TopBraid Composer historically, Protégé and bespoke OMG tooling currently). The reason is institutional: FIBO's authors are subject-matter ontologists, not data engineers; the cycle time on a class definition is days, not minutes; and the editorial review is granular per-term. I concede that FIBO's pattern would be wrong for OPDA. The PDTF v3 schema has 935 annotated leaves — the mechanical half is exactly the case where ontology-IDE-driven authoring underdelivers and Allemang's generator-first approach overdelivers. The cycle time matters: a small change to a glossary term cascades to dozens of mechanical leaves, and the generator handles that automatically while a hand-author would have to chase down each one. The generator handles the mechanical; Council and module-ODR cycles handle the genuinely ambiguous.

One FIBO learning worth carrying: the boundary between "mechanical" and "genuinely ambiguous" is itself the locus of dispute. In FIBO we found that what one ontologist treated as mechanical (a 1:1 datatype property from a schema field) another treated as ambiguous (the schema field had hidden semantics — say, an enumeration encoding a state-machine state, or a string field that was actually a structured identifier). The discipline that emerged: every "this is mechanical" claim has to be backed by a glossary entry confirming the term's intent is purely scalar. If the glossary doesn't have an entry, the leaf is not mechanical and goes to a module ODR. OPDA's 935 annotated leaves are exactly where this discipline applies — the *annotation* is what makes the leaf mechanical-eligible; the 622 un-annotated leaves go to module ODRs first.

### Q6 — Diagnostic-exemplar policy

**Pair joint position:** The three canonical exemplars from S001 Q1 (registered freehold house; unregistered house pre-first-registration; flat whose UPRN was split) are the right starting set. The storage convention should follow Allemang's lead — these are IC test cases, not deliverable artefacts, and they live in an `exemplars/` subdirectory keyed to the IC they test, not in the main TTL output. Each exemplar is a small Turtle file containing the individual's triples plus the SHACL constraint(s) it is designed to falsify (or satisfy).

The harness pattern worth borrowing from FIBO's test suite: each exemplar file declares (a) the individual under test, (b) the SHACL constraint targeted, and (c) the *expected verdict* (pass / fail / specific report contents). The CI runs the SHACL processor and verifies the verdict matches. This makes the exemplar self-documenting and the test self-checking — a maintainer reading the file sees what's being tested and what should happen. Counter-pattern: exemplars that exercise the SHACL machinery but don't declare their expected outcome, leaving the test infrastructure to record verdicts as oracle without anyone verifying the oracle is right.

**Kendall:** The filename should match the exemplar's natural-language description — `registered-freehold-house.ttl`, `unregistered-pre-first-registration.ttl`, `split-uprn-flat.ttl`. This is the FIBO test-case-naming discipline (FIBO MTP §5.2): the filename is the documentation. Counter-pattern: cryptic `ic-test-001.ttl` filenames that force the maintainer to open the file to discover what it tests. The filename-as-documentation rule has saved FIBO test-suite maintenance for over a decade.

**Davis:** And the exemplars themselves should be small — under 50 lines of Turtle each, ideally — so they read like a worked example, not a fixture. BBC's RDF test suite followed this rule explicitly: every test was a hand-readable Turtle file that a maintainer could understand in one screen. Large exemplars become opaque; small exemplars stay legible. For OPDA's three canonical exemplars, this is achievable — each one is a single property, a couple of role attachments, and the SHACL constraints under test. The S005 IC discipline will surface more exemplars as the hard cases multiply (HMO leases, shared-ownership leases, leasehold-with-managed-area-rights); the small-file discipline should scale with the harness.

### Q7 — Namespace string + version scheme (DAVIS DEPTH)

**Davis:** This is a UK-gov-style publish-first decision, and it splits into two sub-decisions the draft correctly leaves to the WG: the *string* and the *versioning scheme*. We take strong positions on both.

**Namespace string.** `opda.uk/ns/` is the right choice IF and only if OPDA owns the domain and commits to long-term resolution. The Linked Data Principles (Berners-Lee 2006, principles 1–2) and the W3C TAG "Cool URIs Don't Change" Note (2008) make the same point: a namespace URI is a long-term commitment, not a branding choice. Once the URI is published and consumers begin to dereference it, changing it imposes a per-consumer renaming cost. The data.gov.uk linked-data cookbook §1 made this the first decision because every subsequent decision depends on it.

`trust.propdata.org.uk/ontology/` carries trust-framework branding and signals OPDA's role as steward of the trust framework — but it *couples the ontology's persistence to the trust-framework programme's persistence*. If OPDA renames or rescopes the trust framework (which is plausible over a 10–20 year horizon — UK gov programmes routinely do), every minted URI breaks. We have lived this at BBC (BBC Music's URLs survived `bbc.co.uk/music/` becoming `bbc.co.uk/radio/...` only because of aggressive 301 redirect maintenance) and at data.gov.uk (departmental rebrandings repeatedly broke departmental URIs that should have been at `data.gov.uk/...` from the start).

The asymmetry is worth pinning: a programme-scoped namespace can always be made narrower (you can publish `opda.uk/trust-framework/ns/` later as a sibling), but you cannot make a programme namespace broader without renaming everything underneath it. Institutional namespaces (the organisation that funds the work) are durable; programme namespaces (the specific deliverable) are not. OPDA the organisation outlasts any one programme it runs; host the durable artefacts at the durable namespace.

Recommendation: **`opda.uk/ns/` for the ontology**, with the trust-framework as a separate, lifecycle-bounded vocabulary in its own namespace if needed (`opda.uk/trust-framework/ns/` or similar). The institutional namespace (OPDA) is more persistent than the programme namespace (trust framework); host the durable artefacts at the durable namespace.

If the WG chooses `trust.propdata.org.uk/ontology/` despite this advice, the mitigation is a strong, documented redirect commitment — any future rename publishes 301s from the old namespace to the new for at least 10 years (the W3C TAG's "Cool URIs" persistence horizon). The cost is non-trivial: the redirect rules have to be maintained even as the OPDA site evolves, and any link in a downstream consumer's database stays valid only as long as the redirect chain is intact. data.gov.uk's experience: redirect chains over a decade old eventually break when the underlying infrastructure migrates, and the breakage is silent — consumers just stop being able to resolve URIs. The institutional namespace avoids the redirect chain entirely.

**Versioning scheme.** Calendar versioning (`2026-q3`, `2026-q4`) is right for an evolving ontology when releases are time-bound and consumers benefit from knowing "this is the Q3 2026 release of the OPDA Foundation". Semantic versioning (`1.0.0`, `1.1.0`) is right when breaking changes are tracked explicitly and consumers depend on compatibility guarantees. The schema project pins `3.4.0` — semver — because the JSON schema is consumed by validators that need to know breaking-change boundaries. For the *ontology*, the consumer pattern is different: consumers dereference the namespace and read the current Turtle; they typically don't pin to a specific version.

Recommendation: **calendar versioning for the Foundation** (`owl:versionIRI` of `opda.uk/ns/2026-q3/` or similar) — releases are time-bound, the Foundation evolves slowly, and the calendar mark is human-readable. **Semantic versioning for individual modules** (ODR-0006 through ODR-0013) when they reach stability — modules MAY carry semver if downstream consumers depend on breaking-change tracking; module ODRs may opt in. This mirrors UK gov practice (data.gov.uk used calendar for the catalogue, semver for individual schemas where consumer contracts existed). FIBO uses an analogous split — semver on individual modules within calendar-versioned releases — which is what Kendall references below.

The published header convention: the Foundation `owl:Ontology` declares both `owl:versionInfo "2026-q3"` (the calendar mark, human-readable) and `owl:versionIRI <opda.uk/ns/2026-q3/>` (the dereferenceable versioned IRI). The bare namespace `opda.uk/ns/` resolves to the current release; the versioned IRI resolves to the snapshot. Consumers who want stability pin to the versioned IRI; consumers who want current track the bare namespace. This is the W3C convention (used by `xsd:`, `rdf:`, `rdfs:`, etc.) and the FIBO convention.

**Kendall:** Concur with Davis's split. FIBO publishes calendar-versioned *releases* (the 2024-Q4 release, the 2025-Q1 release) and semver-versioned *individual modules* within those releases — both schemes coexist because they serve different consumers. The release calendar is for the integration teams who pull the whole production roll; the module semver is for the application teams who consume a specific module and need to know if a class signature has changed in a breaking way (FIBO MTP §6.1, FIBO Release Management Process). The discipline is: calendar for the platform; semver for the modules; the two are reconciled by the release manifest mapping every module's semver at the moment of each calendar release. Applied to OPDA: calendar `owl:versionIRI` on the Foundation; semver allowed on modules at module-ODR discretion.

One FIBO discipline worth porting: every release carries an explicit *release manifest* listing every module's semver at the moment of release. The manifest is itself dereferenceable (FIBO publishes `release-manifest-2024Q4.ttl`); it enumerates module IRIs and pins each to its module-version IRI. For OPDA, the equivalent would be a `release-manifest-2026-q3.ttl` at `opda.uk/ns/2026-q3/manifest`. Integration consumers read the manifest to know which module versions belong to the release; application consumers read individual module IRIs directly. The manifest is the bridge between calendar and semver.

## Replies to anticipated DA attacks

- **Knublauch (DA) will attack Q3 composition as too abstract.** Reply: build-step composition is operationally trivial — `cat foundation.ttl shapes.ttl > combined.ttl` is the literal instantiation. The load-bearing case is *profile composition* (where multiple overlay profiles compose into a single SHACL run), and that is ODR-0010's territory, not Foundation's. The Foundation just declares that the two graphs live in separate artefacts and that no `owl:imports` crosses the boundary. Knublauch's SHACL processors all consume concatenated datasets; this is the standard pattern, not an abstraction. Concrete proof: TopBraid Composer (Knublauch's own tool) reads class graphs and shapes graphs from separate files into a single in-memory dataset for SHACL validation — the build-step composition is what his own tooling does.

- **Knublauch (DA) will attack Q5 generator output as not reproducible.** Reply: deterministic generator + checked-in output IS reproducibility. The reproducibility test is: run the generator on commit N of the data dictionary, compare the output to the committed TTL at commit N; if they differ, the build fails. This is exactly the discipline the BBC `/programmes/` nightly build enforced and that data.gov.uk's DCAT generator inherited. The non-reproducibility failure mode (generator runs differently on different hosts) is preventable by pinning the generator's environment (Python version, library versions) — a CI-level concern, not an ontology-level one. The objection conflates "generator output that diverges from committed output" (a CI bug — preventable) with "generator-first is non-reproducible" (the design — robustly reproducible when CI is honest).

- **Knublauch (DA) may attack Q4 precedence as un-checkable.** Reply: the precedence is mechanically lintable. Every minted term carries `dct:source`; the linter checks that, for terms in the W3C-governed list (`cred:`, `did:`, `prov:`, `dct:`, etc.), the `dct:source` resolves to the W3C spec, not to the glossary; for glossary-governed terms, the linter checks the glossary row exists; for schema-fallback terms, the linter checks the canonical-leaf path exists. This is the FIBO `fibo-fnd-utl-av:adaptedFrom` lint applied to OPDA. Pre-flight scope check material.

- **Knublauch (DA) may attack Q7 namespace choice as out-of-scope for the Foundation.** Reply: the Foundation explicitly leaves the literal namespace string and versioning scheme as WG-owned (Rule 1 and the Open questions reference). What we're offering is a *strong default recommendation* the WG can act on quickly; the Foundation ODR codifies that the *policy* is fixed (single hash namespace, calendar+semver split per the FIBO/UK-gov precedent), and the WG fills in the strings. This matches S001 Q7's spike-then-scale framing: get the policy locked, fill in the strings, ship.

- **Guarino (DA via S001 reflex) may attack Q3 as conflating reasoning-graph and validation-graph theories.** Reply: the three-graph separation is precisely the response to that critique. The OWL graph carries open-world entailment semantics; the SHACL graph carries closed-world constraint semantics; the annotation graph carries presentational semantics. Keeping them in separate artefacts and forbidding `owl:imports` across the boundary IS the operational expression of keeping the theories separate. The SHACL processor reads both class+shapes for validation; the OWL reasoner reads only classes; neither has to manage the other's vocabulary. This is the FIBO Production-profile pattern Guarino has historically endorsed.

- **Cagle (DA, via S001 dissent on `aiHint`) may attack Q3's exile of annotations as preventing AI-consumer access.** Reply: the annotation graph is dereferenceable and consumers explicitly load it when they need annotations. The separation does not prevent access; it prevents *contamination* of the class graph by annotations that an OWL reasoner would otherwise have to filter. AI consumers wanting `opda:aiHint` load the annotation graph alongside the class graph; they get full access. The discipline is "annotations are loaded by consumers that want them, not by reasoners that don't" — the same discipline Cagle's own SHACL-AI work in *The Ontologist* applies to DASH UI hints.

## Pair cross-talk: where Kendall and Davis converge and diverge

We are mostly aligned. The two genuine cross-cutting differences worth recording for the Queen:

**Convergence on the substance.** Both perspectives endorse: hash namespace at TBox scale (Q1); flat namespace with naming-convention enforcement (Q2); build-step graph composition with no `owl:imports` across boundaries (Q3); W3C > glossary > schema-leaf precedence (Q4); generator-first for the mechanical half (Q5); diagnostic exemplars as small, self-documenting test cases (Q6); institutional namespace with calendar+semver split (Q7).

**Divergence on cycle time.** Kendall's FIBO instinct treats Council and module-ODR cycles as the appropriate locus for term-by-term review — the cycle time is days per term, and the editorial discipline is what produces quality. Davis's UK-gov instinct treats publish-first as the appropriate discipline — ship a sketch, iterate against real consumers, accept that the model will be refactored as deployment surfaces real requirements. For OPDA's Foundation, the tension resolves by scope: the Foundation is small enough that Kendall's editorial discipline applies (it IS the substrate every module ODR inherits, so quality matters); but the *module* rollout should follow Davis's publish-first pattern (ship the BASPI5 vertical slice, learn from consumers, iterate). This is what S001 Q7's MVP framing already adopted; we re-endorse it.

**Divergence on hand-authoring vs generator.** Kendall's FIBO experience favours hand-authoring under editorial review. Davis's BBC/data.gov.uk experience favours generator-first with checked-in output. For OPDA's 935-annotated-leaf mechanical half, Davis's pattern wins because the cycle time and the volume both favour generation. For the genuinely ambiguous aggregate boundaries (Property vs LegalEstate; Participant-as-Role vs Participant-as-Kind; oneOf-as-subclass-vs-state), Kendall's hand-authoring under module ODRs wins because the deliberation matters. The split is along S001 Q1's mechanical/ambiguous boundary.

## Pair recommendations for ODR-0004 amendments

We endorse the draft as written and suggest two textual amendments the Queen may carry into synthesis:

1. **Rule 7 (Term-sourcing & provenance): add conflict-logging discipline.** Append after the existing precedence statement: *"Conflicts between sources are recorded as Change Log rows in the relevant module ODR, with the divergence explained and the precedence applied. The glossary row, if it diverges from the W3C source, carries `rdfs:seeAlso` to the canonical source."* This codifies FIBO MTP Ch. 4 inside OPDA's own Rules.

2. **References (Open questions): add concrete recommendation language.** The current draft frames `opda.uk/ns/` vs `trust.propdata.org.uk/ontology/` as a neutral WG choice; we recommend appending a sentence noting the pair's strong default: *"Recommendation to WG (Kendall+Davis, S004): institutional namespace (`opda.uk/ns/`) is preferred over programme namespace on cool-URI persistence grounds; calendar versioning recommended for Foundation, semver permitted at module level."* This gives the WG a concrete default to act on or override; it doesn't bind the WG.

We do NOT recommend changing the core rules (1–6, 8) — they are correctly drafted and our positions are endorsements, not amendments.
