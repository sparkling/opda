# Market Research: Digital Twins, Linked Data, and Agentic Retail

## Executive Summary

Digital twins in fashion retail are still early. Most public retail examples are
store twins, supply-chain control towers, RFID-enabled inventory systems,
planning platforms, or AI copilots. Few retailers publicly describe a true
enterprise digital twin that connects product lifecycle, inventory, supplier
capacity, logistics, channels, sustainability, and execution.

That absence is important. It means H&M should not wait for a mature fashion
retail pattern. The transferable evidence comes from four adjacent domains:

- Retailers using real-time inventory, store technology, and agentic AI.
- Logistics and supply chain operators using twins for visibility and scenario
  planning.
- Industrial companies using digital twins for product and production lifecycle.
- Ontology-led operating models that connect business entities, decisions, and
  actions.

The strategic implication is that H&M can create a category-defining pattern:
a fashion enterprise twin where a linked-data enterprise model is the world
model and agents become the operating layer.

## Retail Evidence

### H&M's Public Direction

H&M Group's own public reporting already points toward the need for this
programme.

In the full-year 2025 report, H&M linked improved product offering to more
efficient purchasing, shorter decision paths, better trend capture, supply chain
improvements, closer supplier cooperation, and better inventory efficiency.

In Q1 2026, H&M reported:

- Net sales of SEK 49.607 billion.
- Gross margin of 50.7 percent.
- Operating profit of SEK 1.512 billion.
- Stock-in-trade down 16 percent to SEK 34.608 billion.
- Stock-in-trade equal to 15.6 percent of rolling 12-month sales.
- Online just over 30 percent of sales.
- 4,050 stores at 28 February 2026.

H&M also publicly describes a complex supply chain:

- Over 554 commercial product suppliers.
- Over 969 tier 1 factories.
- China and Bangladesh as the largest clothing production markets.
- Around 1.1 million people employed by tier 1 and tier 2 supplier factories.
- 12 local production offices with over 1,300 colleagues working with suppliers.

These facts support a linked-data approach. The operating system has many
entities and relationships: products, factories, suppliers, workers, materials,
orders, stores, channels, shipments, compliance requirements, and lifecycle
events.

### Inditex

Inditex is the most relevant fashion comparator because its operating advantage
has historically been speed, store integration, and supply chain responsiveness.

Its 2025 annual report emphasises:

- Physical stores and online platforms as mutually reinforcing.
- Store technology such as Click & Collect silos, sorters, assisted
  self-checkouts, pick-up points, and new alarm technology.
- AI-enabled Try-On in 26 markets.
- 5,460 stores across 97 markets at fiscal year end.

The transferable lesson is not "copy Inditex's systems". It is that technology
is embedded in the operating model: stores, online, logistics, product, and
customer experience are treated as one system.

### Walmart

Walmart is not a fashion comparator, but it is a strong signal for AI-native
retail operations.

Walmart publicly describes:

- Real-time AI and automation in its global supply chain.
- Reusable platforms such as self-healing inventory and agentic AI.
- Tools that help teams respond when a store receives less product than
  expected, turning analysis into recommended next steps.
- Purpose-built agentic AI tools for retail-specific tasks.

The transferable lesson is that agentic AI is moving from experimentation into
core retail operations. H&M should assume leading retailers will use agents to
coordinate inventory, supply, pricing, stores, and customer experience.

### Lowe's

Lowe's built a store digital twin using NVIDIA Omniverse. Its public
announcement says the twin combines spatial data, product location, and
historical order information, and can support associates in store operations.

The transferable lesson is that a twin is most useful when it connects spatial,
operational, and commercial data into a decision environment. For H&M, the 3D
store aspect is less central than the principle: a twin should align the
physical and digital reality of operations.

### Shein as Threat Model

Shein is controversial and should not be treated as a model to copy wholesale.
However, it is an important threat model for fashion responsiveness.

Public reporting and analysis commonly describe Shein's model as small-batch,
data-driven, and digitally coordinated with suppliers. The transferable lesson
is that the market is rewarding the ability to sense demand, produce or
replenish in small batches, and react quickly.

For H&M, the strategic challenge is to combine responsiveness with brand,
quality, sustainability, compliance, stores, and global scale. A linked-data
enterprise twin can help H&M compete on speed without reducing the company to a
pure ultra-fast-fashion model.

## Supply Chain and Logistics Evidence

### McKinsey

McKinsey defines a digital twin as a virtual replica of a physical object,
person, system, or process, linked to real data and used for simulation and
decision-making.

McKinsey's supply chain digital twin work highlights:

- Real-time visibility into in-transit inventory, customer journeys, and
  staffing.
- Supply chain disruption costs that can be very material.
- Decision-speed improvements from digital-twin-enabled supply chains.
- Examples where systems twins reduced transportation cost and labour and
  improved customer promise.
- A retail supply chain digital twin example with improved regional DC
  utilisation and lower fulfilment cost.

The transferable lesson is that a twin should be built around decisions and
trade-offs, not around visualisation.

### BCG

BCG's value-chain digital twin work describes combining internal demand,
supply, inventory, and consumption data with external signals such as industry
indices, port utilisation, and shipping volumes.

BCG reports early adopter benefits including:

- 20-30 percent forecast accuracy improvement.
- Broad planning coverage, including long-tail items.
- 50-80 percent reductions in delays and downtime in cited industrial contexts.

The transferable lesson for H&M is to connect internal and external signals in
one operating model, especially where geopolitical risk, tariffs, port issues,
supplier risk, and shifting demand affect decisions.

### Maersk

Maersk describes supply chain digital twins as connecting suppliers,
warehouses, distribution centres, products, and customers, with uses including
inventory, warehousing, product lifecycle, new product introduction, and reverse
logistics.

The transferable lesson is that product lifecycle and logistics should not be
separate twin domains. They interact directly in fashion through launch timing,
availability, returns, resale, and end-of-life.

## Semantic Operating Model Patterns

### Palantir

Palantir's Ontology is relevant because it frames the enterprise model as more
than data. It represents objects, logic, actions, and operational workflows, so
humans and AI agents can collaborate around decisions.

Palantir should not be treated here as a database vendor or as a recommended
technical substrate. The useful comparison is at the operating-model level:
how an enterprise ontology links data, business logic, action semantics,
permissions, workflows, and AI decision support.

The relevant lesson is architectural:

- Model the business entities.
- Model the relationships.
- Model the logic.
- Model the actions.
- Govern access and write-back.

This aligns with the proposed H&M direction, but the goal is not to outsource
the H&M operating model to a third-party platform. H&M should build its own
linked-data-based digital twin and custom graph capability, using open-source
patterns and selectively chosen components where they accelerate delivery.

## Capability Categories To Benchmark

This research pack should not become a graph-platform vendor comparison. For a
board and digital-leadership audience, the useful comparison is capability-led:

| Capability | Relevance to H&M |
|---|---|
| Enterprise semantic model | Shared business meaning for products, suppliers, factories, stores, channels, inventory, lifecycle, decisions, and actions |
| Decision and action ontology | Explicit representation of recommendations, approvals, overrides, write-backs, outcomes, and learning |
| Supply chain planning and optimisation | Benchmarks for allocation, replenishment, routing, supplier capacity, markdown, and scenario planning |
| Agent runtime and evaluation | Governed role-specific agents that can query, simulate, recommend, execute within policy, and be measured |
| Event and state management | Live operational state, history, confidence, provenance, and decision traceability |
| Open-source linked-data tooling | Strategic fit with H&M-owned semantics and reduced dependence on a closed operating-model vendor |

## Lessons for H&M

1. Fashion retail has no dominant enterprise twin pattern yet. H&M can define
   one.
2. Linked data is the right strategic modelling foundation because the problem
   is relationships:
   product-to-material, SKU-to-store, supplier-to-factory, factory-to-capacity,
   shipment-to-route, campaign-to-demand, action-to-outcome.
3. Agents need a governed world model. Without it, they will not be reliable
   enough for operational decisions.
4. The twin must connect to actions. Visibility without action is another
   dashboard.
5. The first use case must have visible P&L impact. Inventory allocation,
   in-season replenishment, and trend response are better starting points than
   abstract data governance.
6. Implementation choices should remain subordinate to H&M's own operating
   model, semantics, decision rights, and action governance.
